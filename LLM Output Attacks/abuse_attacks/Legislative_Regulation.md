### Legislative Regulation 

Governments are increasingly introducing regulations to address LLM abuse attacks such as misinformation, hate speech, and deepfakes. A major challenge in regulating LLMs is balancing **innovation and accountability** while protecting **fundamental rights like freedom of speech**. Another key issue is determining **liability**—whether responsibility lies with model developers, deployers, or end users who generate harmful content.

---

### Regulation in the United States

In the U.S., misinformation is generally protected under **free speech laws**, unless it involves **defamation, fraud, or incitement to violence**, making regulation difficult.

Key approaches include:

- **Take It Down Act**
  - Targets abuse involving **AI-generated deepfakes**, particularly **non-consensual intimate imagery**.
  - Criminalizes the distribution of certain abusive AI-generated content.

- **NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF)**
  - Provides voluntary guidelines for developing **trustworthy and responsible AI systems**.
  - Encourages organizations to manage risks associated with AI misuse.

- **Federal Trade Commission (FTC) oversight**
  - The FTC can intervene if AI systems are used in **fraudulent or deceptive business practices**.

Overall, the U.S. regulatory approach focuses more on **existing legal frameworks and voluntary best practices**.

---

### Regulation in the European Union

The EU has implemented stronger and more structured regulation through two major laws:

#### Digital Services Act (DSA)

The **DSA** regulates digital platforms and requires them to:

- Provide **mechanisms for reporting illegal content**
- **Remove illegal content** when reported
- Offer **appeal systems** for wrongly removed content
- Conduct **risk assessments** on issues like misinformation and cyber violence
- Improve **content moderation and algorithm transparency**

The DSA applies to **all digital services operating in the EU**, even if the provider is based outside the EU.

Goals of the DSA include:
- Protecting users from harmful content
- Safeguarding fundamental rights
- Increasing transparency
- Strengthening platform accountability

---

#### EU Artificial Intelligence Act (AI Act)

The **AI Act** specifically regulates AI systems using a **risk-based classification model**:

- **Unacceptable-risk AI**
  - Includes manipulative or harmful systems such as **social scoring**
  - **Completely banned**

- **High-risk AI**
  - Used in critical sectors like **healthcare, education, or law enforcement**
  - Requires strict regulations including **risk management, governance, and human oversight**

- **Limited-risk AI**
  - Includes **LLMs and AI systems that generate content**
  - Requires **transparency obligations**, such as:
    - Disclosure when content is AI-generated
    - Safeguards to prevent misuse

- **Minimal-risk AI**
  - Includes systems like **spam filters or video games**
  - Largely **unregulated**

---

### Key Takeaway

Legislative efforts in the U.S. and EU aim to **limit AI abuse while preserving innovation and free speech**.  

- **U.S. approach:** relies more on existing laws, enforcement agencies, and voluntary frameworks.  
- **EU approach:** uses comprehensive regulatory frameworks (DSA and AI Act) with **risk-based AI classification and stricter transparency requirements**.
